## THE BREAKING YARD

**1. DERIVATION LEAPS** — Authors skip steps. Those skipped steps are where the errors live.
- Trace the derivation from Equation N to N+1. Is there a step that is not mathematically justified?
- Does the author claim a result "follows trivially" when it actually requires unstated (and perhaps invalid) assumptions?
- Are there "load-bearing" equations that the entire paper rests on? Check them for basic algebraic errors.

**2. INDEPENDENT RE-DERIVATION** — Do the math yourself. Do NOT just read their math and nod along.
- For key results (main theorems, central equations, headline formulas), RE-DERIVE the result from the paper's own stated premises and definitions.
- Compare your result term-by-term against the paper's published expression. Any discrepancy in coefficient, sign, index, or functional form is a finding.
- Pay particular attention to change-of-variables derivations: check that Jacobians are computed correctly and that all prefactors carry through.
- Check proof steps individually: verify that each line follows from the preceding one, with correct subscripts, indices, and conditioning arguments.
- **VERIFY EVERY CLAIM THAT AN EQUATION MATCHES AN EXISTING CONSTRUCTION.** When the paper says "this matrix is the X of construction Y" or "this corresponds to the chain associated with Z" or "this is the kernel for [some named process]", DO NOT take it on trust. Build construction Y or chain Z from the paper's own description (and your own knowledge of the construction if needed) and compare term by term against the paper's equation. If the relative weights of any two components differ by even a constant factor, that is a finding. Authors paper over factor-of-2 errors in this kind of identification all the time, because nobody re-derives the matching construction.

**3. SIGN ERRORS & COMPARATIVE STATICS** — The most common errors in published papers.
- The paper claims an effect is "increasing in $X$". Does the derivative with respect to $X$ actually produce a positive value? COMPUTE IT.
- Check for sign errors in the final results of derivations.
- When a variable is defined earlier in the paper and then used in a later equation, verify that the sign convention is consistent.
- **Check footnotes too.** If a footnote claims a ratio is increasing or decreasing in a parameter, COMPUTE THE DERIVATIVE AND VERIFY THE SIGN. Footnotes are where authors are laziest.

**4. RECURSION & ITERATION VERIFICATION** — Expand, don't trust.
- For recursive formulas, trace the recursion to its terminal or boundary case. Does the stated boundary value match what the recursion produces when iterated to that point?
- When a recursion is presented alongside its explicit product form, EXPAND THE RECURSION step by step and compare term by term against the product. Check every index.
- For importance sampling or iterative estimators, check that the weight formula is consistent with the ratio of target to proposal densities.

**5. OPTIMIZATION CLAIMS** — Authors claim optima. Authors are WRONG about their optima.
- The paper says "$x^*$ minimizes $f$". DID THEY CHECK? A critical point is NOT a minimum just because the first-order condition is satisfied. COMPUTE THE SECOND DERIVATIVE (or the Hessian for multivariate problems) AND VERIFY.
- The paper claims a function is convex. VERIFY IT. For scalar functions, check $f'' \geq 0$. For multivariate functions, check the Hessian is positive semidefinite. "It looks convex" is NOT a proof. DO THE MATH.
- Constrained optimization: are the KKT conditions applied correctly? Does complementary slackness actually hold? Did they check a constraint qualification (Slater's condition, LICQ, etc.) or just assume one?
- The paper claims uniqueness of an optimum. Is the objective strictly convex/concave? If not, WHERE IS THE UNIQUENESS ARGUMENT?
- Saddle points, minimax problems, game-theoretic equilibria: is the critical point actually the right TYPE of critical point? A saddle point is not a minimum. CHECK.

**6. PROOF LOGIC** — Does this proof prove what it CLAIMS to prove?
- **CONVERSE ERROR.** The most insidious proof bug. The paper needs to show A implies B. Does the proof actually show B implies A? This is NOT the same thing. Read the proof's logical structure, not just its algebra. If the proof starts from the conclusion and works backward to the premises, IT IS PROVING THE CONVERSE.
- **CIRCULAR REASONING.** Does the proof assume its own conclusion? This can be subtle: the result is sometimes smuggled in through a "without loss of generality" step or an "it is easy to see that" hand-wave. If you strip away those phrases, does the argument still stand?
- **NECESSARY vs SUFFICIENT.** The paper proves a condition is necessary for X. Then it uses that condition as if it were sufficient for X. THESE ARE DIFFERENT THINGS.
- **QUANTIFIER ERRORS.** The paper proves "there exists an $x$ such that P(x)." Then it uses this as if it proved "for all $x$, P(x)." Or vice versa. Authors confuse "for all" and "there exists" ALL THE TIME, especially in proofs involving convergence, approximation, or equilibrium existence.
- **UNIQUENESS WITHOUT PROOF.** The paper claims the solution is unique, but the proof only establishes existence. Where is the uniqueness argument?
