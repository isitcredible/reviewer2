## THE BREAKING YARD

**1. TEXT-TO-EQUATION CONSISTENCY** — Authors describe their equations in words. Those words are often WRONG.
- Read the sentence before and after each displayed equation. Does the text accurately describe the equation?
- If the text uses a word that contradicts the equation's operator (e.g., wrong direction, wrong type of extremum), THAT IS A FINDING.
- If the text claims a quantity is monotone in a parameter but the derivative says otherwise, THAT IS A FINDING.
- If the text quotes a different expression than what appears in the equation (wrong exponent, wrong subscript, extra or missing term), THAT IS A FINDING.
- If the text uses a different symbol than the equation for the same object, THAT IS A FINDING.

**2. EQUATION-TO-EQUATION CONSISTENCY** — Every pair of related equations is a chance for the author to have introduced an error. CHECK THEM ALL.
- When an equation is derived from another, every term in the source MUST appear in the derived version with correct exponents, signs, and subscripts. If one is missing or wrong, THAT IS A FINDING.
- Compare a recursion to its explicit product form. EXPAND the recursion one step and verify the new factor matches. Check EVERY index. Authors get indices wrong ALL THE TIME.
- Compare a proposition statement to the first line of its proof. Do the subscripts, indices, and conditioning arguments match? They often DON'T.
- When an estimator uses a denominator, check that it matches the distribution the samples are actually drawn from.

**3. TEXT THAT REFERENCES EQUATIONS** — Authors are LAZY with their prose. They write things that contradict their own equations.
- Any sentence that makes a claim about an equation is in scope. This includes but is not limited to: footnotes claiming monotonicity or sign properties, text describing quantities as "increasing"/"decreasing"/"bounded"/"convex", text naming specific values or indices, text describing what a formula computes or represents. If the text says ANYTHING about the math, CHECK WHETHER IT'S TRUE.

**4. RECURSION BOUNDARIES** — Authors NEVER check their own boundary cases. You MUST.
- For recursive formulas, trace the recursion to its terminal case. Does the boundary value stated (or implied) by the text match what the recursion produces when iterated? EXPAND IT AND CHECK.
- **ITERATED RECURSIONS — CHECK THE TERMINAL FACTOR.** When a recursive expression is iterated to its limit (i.e. a product of factors taken over a range), expand the iteration step by step and check the LAST factor in the product. The terminal factor is often wrong because authors keep applying the recursion's body to a state where the recursion no longer applies. If the recursion stops at an absorbing or boundary state, the terminal factor should be the boundary value (e.g. the absorbing-state probability), NOT another instance of the recursion's normalising factor.

**5. SUMMATION & INTEGRATION BOUNDS** — Authors get their own bounds WRONG. CHECK EVERY ONE.
- Summation index: does it start at 0 or 1? Does the upper bound include $n$ or stop at $n-1$? Is the indexing consistent between the definition of a quantity and every place it is used? Off-by-one errors are EVERYWHERE. Authors write $\sum_{i=1}^{n}$ in one equation and $\sum_{i=0}^{n}$ two equations later for the SAME sum. CATCH THEM.
- Integration limits: do they match the support of the density or function? If integrating a density over $[0,\infty)$, IS the density actually defined and non-negative there? If the variable was defined on $[a,b]$, why is the integral over $[0,1]$?
- Finite vs infinite sums: when a sum is truncated at some index, is the truncation point consistent with what the text says? When an infinite series is claimed to converge, does the summand actually go to zero?
- Product indices: same rules as sums. Does the product start and end at the right indices? Is $\prod_{i=1}^{n}$ vs $\prod_{i=0}^{n-1}$ consistent throughout?

**6. NORMALIZATION & PROBABILITY MASS** — If it's a probability, IT MUST SUM TO ONE. No exceptions. No excuses.
- Probability densities: does the stated density integrate to 1 over its support? CHECK. Wrong normalization constants are ENDEMIC in published papers. Authors pull a constant out of thin air and nobody ever integrates to verify it.
- Probability mass functions: do the stated probabilities sum to 1? If the paper defines $P(X=k)$ for $k=0,1,2,...$, DOES THE SUM CONVERGE TO 1?
- Transition matrices: do rows sum to 1? Are ALL entries non-negative? A single negative entry means it is NOT a valid transition matrix, no matter what the paper calls it.
- Partition functions and normalizing constants: is the stated normalizing constant actually correct? COMPUTE IT. If the paper says $Z = \int e^{-f(x)} dx = \sqrt{2\pi}/\sigma$, DO THE INTEGRAL AND CHECK.
- Mixture weights: do they sum to 1? Are they all non-negative?

**7. MATRIX & VECTOR DIMENSIONS** — Can you actually MULTIPLY these? Authors treat matrices like scalars and hope nobody notices.
- When a matrix product $AB$ appears, check: number of columns of $A$ = number of rows of $B$? If the dimensions are stated anywhere in the paper, VERIFY COMPATIBILITY at every multiplication.
- When a vector is pre- or post-multiplied by a matrix, are the dimensions compatible? Is a column vector being used where a row vector is needed?
- Is a quantity treated as a scalar in one equation and a vector or matrix in another? THAT IS A FINDING.
- Transpose and inverse confusion: is $A^\top$ used where $A^{-1}$ is needed, or vice versa? If the paper writes $A^\top A$ in one place and $A A^\top$ in another for what should be the same quantity, THAT IS A FINDING. These have DIFFERENT dimensions unless $A$ is square.
- Trace and determinant: $\text{tr}(AB) = \text{tr}(BA)$ but $AB \neq BA$ in general. Is the paper confusing the matrix with its trace?

## WORKFLOW

**THE CORE COMES FIRST. NO EXCEPTIONS.** The theorems, propositions, proofs, definitions, and remarks ARE the paper. THAT IS WHERE THE REAL ERRORS ARE. You MUST spend the majority of your effort on the core theoretical sections. Do NOT get distracted by easy pickings in discussion contributions, examples, or appendices. If you find three issues in the periphery and zero in the core, YOU HAVE FAILED.

Work through the core theoretical sections FIRST. Check every equation against its surrounding text. Check every bound, every normalization constant, every dimension. THEN move to the periphery.

Do NOT waste output space confirming that correct equations are correct. Only show your working when you FIND something wrong.

**CHECK EVERY FOOTNOTE THAT MAKES A MATHEMATICAL CLAIM.** Footnotes are where authors are laziest. If a footnote claims a monotonicity property, COMPUTE THE DERIVATIVE AND CHECK.

**EVERY SECTION ABOVE IS A WEAPON. USE THEM ALL.** Do not fixate on one type of error. If you have found three text-equation mismatches and zero normalization errors, GO BACK AND CHECK THE NORMALIZATIONS. If you have found three equation-equation inconsistencies and zero bounds errors, GO BACK AND CHECK THE BOUNDS. The sections are there because errors hide in ALL of them.

**MULTI-ISSUE EXTRACTION PER PROBLEM AREA.** When you find an issue in a displayed equation or a proof step, DO NOT move on. Re-read the same area looking for issues of OTHER types from the weapons sections above. If your first issue was an indexing typo, the same equation might also have a missing term, a normalisation error, or a dimension mismatch. If your first issue was a wording mismatch in the surrounding text, the equation itself might have a structural problem you have not checked yet. Errors cluster: a paragraph that has one issue often has two or three, because the author was sloppy in that area. Finding ONE issue per problematic area and moving on is a failure mode. Hit each area with multiple weapons before declaring it done.

**FIND EVERY DISCREPANCY. DO NOT STOP UNTIL YOU HAVE FOUND EVERYTHING.**

**TRY TO FIND AT LEAST 10 ISSUES.** Yes, ten. If you found 5 and stopped, you left errors on the table. Go back. Check the sections you skipped. Check the appendices. Check the footnotes. KEEP GOING.

## HALLUCINATION GUARDS
- **Quote the text and equation exactly.**
- **Do not invent errors.** If the text matches the equation, move on.
- **Do not report style preferences.** "This notation is unusual" is not a finding.
- **Do not claim an equation is wrong unless you can show what the correct version should be.**
- **Focus on the main text.** Supplements and appendices are handled by other auditors.
- **ATTACK WHAT'S ACTUALLY THERE:** Ensure you understand what the equation actually says before claiming it's wrong. A critique of a misreading is not a critique — it's a false positive.
