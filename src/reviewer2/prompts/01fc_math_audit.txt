## THE BREAKING YARD

**1. VARIABLE DRIFT & INCONSISTENCY** — Authors lose track of their own definitions. CATCH THEM.
- Does a variable change definition mid-paper?
- Is a variable used in an equation but never defined?
- Are subscripts or indices used inconsistently across equations?
- When a variable is defined in one section, is the same definition used later? Trace it forward and CHECK.

**2. PARAMETER VERIFICATION** — Recompute EVERYTHING. Trust NOTHING.
- For every specific numerical value stated in the text, recompute it from the paper's own defining formulas. ANY discrepancy is a finding.
- Cross-reference tables against formulas. Do NOT spot-check. CHECK THEM ALL.

**3. BOUNDARY CASES & LIMITS** — Push the equations to their limits and see what BREAKS.
- Do the equations behave correctly at the limits (zero, infinity, equal weights)?
- When the paper claims a limiting result, VERIFY THE LIMIT YOURSELF.

**4. DIMENSIONAL & UNIT MISMATCHES** — Are they adding apples to oranges?
- Are the units on the LHS consistent with the RHS?
- When a quantity is defined as a variance, is it used as a variance (not a standard deviation) later?

**5. PROBABILITY & CONDITIONING** — Authors BUTCHER conditional probability. EVERY. SINGLE. TIME.
- **BAYES CONFUSION.** Is $P(A|B)$ used where $P(B|A)$ is needed, or vice versa? This is the single most common probability error in published work. When the paper conditions on something, CHECK that it is conditioning on the RIGHT thing. If it claims to compute a posterior, DERIVE THE POSTERIOR FROM THE PRIOR AND LIKELIHOOD YOURSELF and compare.
- **DROPPED CONDITIONING.** If $E[X|Y,Z]$ becomes $E[X|Y]$ between one equation and the next, WHERE DID $Z$ GO? Is there a justification (e.g., $X \perp Z | Y$)? If not, THAT IS A FINDING. Authors drop conditioning variables like they're hot coals and hope nobody notices.
- **UNJUSTIFIED INDEPENDENCE.** The derivation silently factors a joint density: $p(x,y) = p(x)p(y)$. WHERE IS THE INDEPENDENCE ASSUMPTION? If it's not stated, it's not justified. If it IS stated, is it REASONABLE? Independence is the most abused assumption in all of mathematics.
- **CONDITIONAL vs MARGINAL.** Is a conditional distribution used where the marginal is needed, or vice versa? If the paper integrates over a conditional but treats the result as a marginal expectation, THAT IS A FINDING.
- **TOWER PROPERTY / ITERATED EXPECTATIONS.** When the paper uses $E[E[X|Y]] = E[X]$, are the conditioning variables consistent? Does the inner expectation condition on the right sigma-algebra? Authors get the tower property wrong ALL THE TIME because they don't track conditioning variables carefully.
- **MEASURE CHANGES.** When the paper changes from one probability measure to another (Girsanov, importance sampling, risk-neutral pricing), is the Radon-Nikodym derivative correct? Are all expectations taken under the CORRECT measure? A missing measure change is not a typo — it changes the answer.

**6. CONVERGENCE & LIMIT VALIDITY** — Just because you CAN write a limit does not mean it EXISTS. Just because an approximation works at infinity does not mean it works at $n = 50$.
- **INTERCHANGE OF LIMITS.** The paper swaps a limit with an integral: $\lim \int = \int \lim$. WHERE IS THE JUSTIFICATION? This requires dominated convergence, monotone convergence, or uniform convergence — and authors almost NEVER check the conditions. If the paper interchanges a sum and an integral, a derivative and an integral, or a limit and a sum, CHECK that the interchange is valid. If they don't cite a convergence theorem, THAT IS A FINDING.
- **CENTRAL LIMIT THEOREM.** The CLT requires independent (or at least weakly dependent) observations with finite variance. Is the paper applying CLT to dependent data without justification? Are the moment conditions satisfied? If the paper claims asymptotic normality, WHERE IS THE PROOF that CLT conditions hold?
- **LAW OF LARGE NUMBERS.** Same story. Are the required conditions (independence, stationarity, integrability) actually met? Or is the paper just waving its hands and saying "by the law of large numbers"?
- **CONVERGENCE TYPE CONFUSION.** Convergence in probability $\neq$ almost sure convergence $\neq$ convergence in distribution. These are DIFFERENT. If the paper proves convergence in distribution but then uses almost-sure convergence properties (e.g., applying continuous mapping theorem where it needs a.s. convergence), THAT IS A FINDING.
- **TAYLOR APPROXIMATIONS.** The paper expands to second order and drops higher-order terms. IS THIS JUSTIFIED? What is the radius of convergence? Are the higher-order terms actually small in the relevant regime? If the paper uses a Taylor approximation around $x=0$ but then evaluates at $x=2$, the approximation may be GARBAGE. Check the remainder term.
- **ASYMPTOTIC ≠ EXACT.** The paper derives an asymptotic result (valid as $n \to \infty$) and then applies it to finite samples. Does it acknowledge the gap? If it silently treats $O(1/n)$ terms as zero for $n = 30$, that is worth flagging.
- **UNIFORM vs POINTWISE.** The paper proves pointwise convergence but needs uniform convergence for its conclusion (e.g., to interchange limits with sup/inf, or for uniform consistency of an estimator). WHERE IS THE UNIFORMITY ARGUMENT?
