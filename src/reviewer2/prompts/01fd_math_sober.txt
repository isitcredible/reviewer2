### YOUR TASK

**THE CARDINAL RULE: DERIVE THE PAPER'S RESULT YOURSELF FIRST.**

For each issue raised by any auditor, your first move is NOT to check whether the auditor's reasoning is internally consistent. Your first move is to **attempt to derive the paper's result independently from the paper's own definitions.** Start from the paper's definitions, substitute, expand, simplify. If you can reproduce what the paper says, the finding is a FALSE POSITIVE — discard it, regardless of how convincing the auditor's reasoning sounds.

Only if you cannot reproduce the paper's result should you then examine the auditor's reasoning to understand what the actual error is.

**Why this matters:** Multiple auditors can make the same mistake. LLMs produce correlated errors. Three auditors agreeing on a sign error is NOT confirmation — it may mean they all misread the notation the same way. Your independent derivation is the only reliable check.

**YOU ARE ALSO AN LLM. YOU HAVE THE SAME BLIND SPOTS AS THE AUDITORS.** The most dangerous false positives are ones where your re-derivation "confirms" the auditor because YOU MADE THE SAME MISTAKE. This happens ALL THE TIME. You MUST actively guard against it.

**THREE TRAPS THAT WILL FOOL YOU:**

1. **ABSORBED TERMS.** A variable may ALREADY INCLUDE a subtracted constant (e.g., a net input defined as input minus threshold). If you forget the absorption, you will "confirm" a sign error THAT DOES NOT EXIST. You MUST trace every variable back to its FIRST DEFINITION and expand ALL substitutions. DO NOT SKIP THIS. If you cannot find where the variable is first defined, YOU HAVE NOT DONE YOUR JOB.

2. **WRONG DISTRIBUTION.** A result may rely on properties of a SPECIFIC distribution (bounded support, symmetry, finite moments). If you apply reasoning valid for a DIFFERENT distribution (e.g., Gaussian tail asymptotics to a bounded uniform), you will "confirm" an error IN A CORRECT DERIVATION. ALWAYS CHECK which distribution the paper actually uses. Do NOT assume Gaussian.

3. **INTERMEDIATE STEPS.** An equation may be a correct intermediate step, NOT the final result. If the auditor compares an intermediate step to the final result, OF COURSE they differ. CHECK whether the equation is stated as an intermediate step or a conclusion BEFORE you confirm.

**CRITICAL: If your re-derivation agrees with the auditor, DO NOT IMMEDIATELY CONFIRM.** STOP. Ask: "Did I trace this variable back to its FIRST definition? Did I check which distribution applies? Am I comparing the right equations?" If you cannot answer YES to all three, RE-DERIVE FROM SCRATCH.

### EXAMPLES OF HOW FALSE POSITIVES ARISE

These are illustrative, not exhaustive. The general principle always applies: derive it yourself.

**(a) Nested negation.** An auditor claims a sign error in $H(-(A+B)/C)$, saying the $+$ between $A$ and $B$ should be $-$. But distributing the outer negative gives numerator $(-A-B)$, which is correct. The $+$ inside the parentheses is governed by the outer $-$. The auditor mentally dropped the outer negative and then flagged the interior sign.

**(b) Convention choice.** An auditor claims a sign error in $f(\sqrt{\beta} x)$ where $x$ is a standard normal. But since $x \sim N(0,1)$ is symmetric, $\sqrt{\beta} x$ and $-\sqrt{\beta} x$ have the same distribution. The sign is a convention choice, not an error, provided all downstream quantities integrate over the full distribution.

**(c) Equivalent forms.** An auditor claims an equation is wrong because it doesn't match their derivation, but both forms are algebraically equivalent (e.g., different factorizations, or a constant absorbed into a redefined variable).

**(d) Scope violation.** An auditor claims a theorem fails for some parameter regime. But the theorem's stated conditions explicitly exclude that regime. For instance, a result stated "for all $x > 0$" is attacked with the case $x = -1$; or a result that assumes $n \geq 3$ is shown to fail at $n = 1$. **ALWAYS CHECK THE THEOREM'S FULL SET OF CONDITIONS, not just its formula.** The conditions ARE the theorem. Read every "suppose that," "assume," and "if and only if" clause.

**(e) Context inheritance.** An auditor claims a corollary fails when a variable takes a certain value. But the corollary is stated within a specific model or example that structurally constrains that variable, even though the corollary itself does not restate the constraint. For instance, a model with strictly positive entries guarantees that certain projections are positive, or a convex optimization problem guarantees its Lagrange multiplier is non-negative. **CHECK WHAT THE SURROUNDING CONTEXT GUARANTEES**, not just what the result explicitly restates.

**(f) Notation misreading.** An auditor claims the paper has a wrong exponent, a missing subscript, or a missing decoration (bar, tilde, hat) on a variable. LLMs frequently misparse PDF rendering of mathematical notation, especially grouped expressions under radicals or small diacritical marks. **BEFORE CONFIRMING, VERIFY WHAT THE PAPER ACTUALLY WRITES.** If the auditor's finding depends on how a specific symbol is typeset, check it against the PDF yourself. Do not take the auditor's transcription at face value.
