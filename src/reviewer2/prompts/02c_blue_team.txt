**TYPES OF ISSUE**

There are seven different types of issue:

**(Type A) Red Team mistake**
- These are the issues where the Red Team's critique depends on a mistake in their description.
- Check that they read the text correctly and have not invented an issue.
- Ensure that they have not invented an issue due to defects in their training data.
- **THE RED TEAM MUST NOT BE ALLOWED TO MAKE ANY MISTAKES.**
- **THE RED TEAM MUST NOT BE ALLOWED TO MISREPRESENT THE TEXT.**
- **Type A issues are the most important type of issue to flag.**
- **ANY** mistake in the Red Team's description must be called out.
- In quantitative texts, any regression results must be correctly interpreted; e.g., look for these kinds of errors when **any** issue involves a regression:
    - Focusing on the reduced form rather than IV coefficient in a 2SLS regression.
    - Mixing coefficients from different regression specifications.
    - Ignoring log transformations.
    - Interpreting logit as if they were OLS.
    - Misinterpreting interaction terms (e.g., treating the coefficient on an interaction as the full marginal effect, or ignoring that constituent terms' interpretation changes)
- Also be careful when the Red Team imputes any specific factual attributes to external sources cited in the text, such as:
    - Years or date ranges
    - Geographic coverage
    - Variables included or excluded
    - Sample restrictions
    - Numerical values
    - Data frequency
    - Methodological procedures
- It is probable that the Red Team has hallucinated these factual attributes to suit its argument.
- For mathematical claims (sign errors, wrong coefficients, incorrect derivations), check the Red Team's algebra carefully. If the Red Team has misquoted an equation or misidentified a variable's definition, flag it as Type A.
- Flag any technical error. Specify whether the Red Team's critique depends on it **partly** or **entirely**.

**(Type B) Acknowledged issues**
- Any issue that the PDF itself recognizes as a potential limitation is an acknowledged issue.
- This does **not** mean that the PDF simply mentions X or Y.
- Rather,the PDF needs to **explicitly acknowledge that X or Y is a potential problem for their argument.**
- Examples:
    - "We acknowledge that X is a limitation..."
    - "We address concern Y in the robustness checks..."
- Be careful: **DO NOT CONFUSE TYPE A ISSUES WITH TYPE E ISSUES**, which are described below. **Just because you find a reference to something in the text, does not mean that the text itself recognizes it as an issue: it may be a feature rather than a bug.**
- **STRICT RULE:

**(Type C) Clerical errors**
- Clerical errors are related to the presentation of information.
- Examples:
    - Sample size discrepancies
    - Unexplained data anomalies
    - Missing references
    - Totals that don't equal the sum
    - Mislabeled figures
- **We CANNOT know the impact of clerical errors, no matter how great they seem.**
- The principle of charity should be applied.

**(Type D) Structural errors**
- The following are **not** clerical errors:
    - Misinterpreted regression coefficients
    - Wrong functional forms
    - Mathematical contradictions in the model's own terms
    - Misinterpretations of theoretical concepts
    - Incorrect derivations
- These are structural issues and cannot be explained away as sloppiness because they threaten the logic of the text.

**(Type E) Visual evidence**
- Visual evidence is strictly forbidden: No issue can be based on the visual interpretation of a figure or plot, or any datapoint from within a figure or plot. Data must come from (a) the text, (b) tables, or (c) figure captions. Interpretations of figures or the extraction of datapoints from within figures are **strictly prohibited**. Do not use this kind of information. If the Red Team used this kind of information in its description, it should be flagged for deletion in the defence.
- **Any** issue that depends on this kind of visual evidence must be immediately dismissed.

**(Type F) Features-not-bugs**
- These issues are not issues at all, but are in fact **intrinsic parts of the text's argument**.
- The Red Team has **misunderstood the text**.
- For example, the Red Team has confused a part of the text's critique of the existing literature for a problem in the text. The Red Team have become confused and have assigned the flaw to the text itself.

**(Type G) Other**
- Any other type of issue.
- **IF THERE IS NO PLAUSIBLE DEFENCE, CLASSIFY IT AS OTHER.**
