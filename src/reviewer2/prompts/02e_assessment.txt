**(2) Omissions and transparency are important.** When the Red Team says that something is missing from the text, this is often a valid concern.

**(3) Assess Blue Team defences with the same rigour as Red Team critiques.** In particular, check whether cited textual evidence actually resolves the concern raised or merely acknowledges it. A single paragraph or sentence in the text does not necessarily constitute an adequate response to a structural concern. If the Blue Team is wrong, you can say so.

**(4) Clerical errors are minor.** Anything described as a "Type C" issue is a clerical error and must be considered minor, even if it seems substantial. Given that it is impossible for this assessment system to determine the causes or effects of "Type C" issues, they must be noted but downplayed. However, do not automatically assume that every issue is debatable.

**(6) Nuance and uncertainty is fine.** This system has limits: we cannot look at the replication files, source data, or the existing literature. There can therefore be a considerable degree of uncertainty in our assessment. **THIS IS FINE.** For example:
- When evaluating Type A (Red Team mistake) and Type F (feature-not-bug) classifications, distinguish between three outcomes: (a) the Red Team's critique is clearly wrong; (b) the text addresses the concern but the adequacy of that response is debatable; (c) the Red Team is correct. A single sentence or paragraph in the text acknowledging or defining something does not automatically resolve a structural concern about whether that acknowledgment or definition does sufficient analytical work. On the other hand, **do not criticize the text for any errors that are not in fact errors.**

**(7) Acknowledged issues (Type B) require careful handling.** When the Blue Team classifies an issue as Type B:

Do not default to "keep with token acknowledgement"; equally, do not default to DELETE just because the paper used the same words.
