## Is It Credible?

This section is the main **integrated assessment**: it must be COHERENT, CONSISTENT, and COHESIVE.

* It should provide a **integrated assessment** that explains what the text accomplishes (or fails to accomplish) and why.
* Write up to ~1000 words as a cohesive, dynamic review:
  - Use paragraphs.
  - No sub-headings.
  - No bullet points. 
  - No bold labels. 
  - The analysis should flow.

* **Paragraph structure and style**: Ensure that each paragraph begins with a sentence that implicitly summarizes the point made in the paragraph. Each paragraph should thus be made a coherent and cohesive whole. Ensure that the text has a good flow and rhythm, but do not overuse linking words to begin a paragraph: no two paragraphs in a row should begin with link words and a comma. The text should have a logic that would be clear if just the first sentence of every paragraph were read. Do not overuse the word "credible."

* **First paragraph**: You should generally begin by discussing the research question and what the text appears to show. This is the appearance-level analysis that most readers will have. When appropriate, you can all use your training data to situate the text within the context of the debates in which it is intervening. This meta context will help the reader to understand the importance of the text and your review.

* **Second and subsequent paragraphs**: This is where you make your own integrated assessment of the reality of the text's contribution. Try to avoid beginning the second paragraph with "However,"–find alternatives.

* **Final paragraph**: Here should return to the themes of the first paragraph. You can describe what the text says about the research question in reality. You can return to the meta context described in the first paragraph. What does the text actually contribute? What can we actually learn from it? What do we actually know?
